What Is Reiki Healing? A Complete Beginner's Guide

If you've heard the word "Reiki" and pictured something vague or mystical, you're not alone — and you're not wrong to want a straight answer before you try it. Here's what Reiki actually is, what happens in a real session, and how to know if it's worth your time.

Reiki, in plain terms

Reiki is a Japanese energy healing technique developed in the early 1900s by Mikao Usui. The word comes from two Japanese characters — rei (universal) and ki (life energy) — and the practice is built on a simple premise: the body carries an energetic field, and when that field is blocked or depleted, it shows up as fatigue, anxiety, tension, or a general sense of being "stuck." A Reiki practitioner channels that universal life energy, through light touch or hands held near the body, to help the field rebalance.

It isn't a religion, and it doesn't require belief to have an effect — though an open, curious mind tends to deepen the experience.

What actually happens in a session

A typical session — whether in-person or via distance healing over video — follows a similar arc:

  • A short intake conversation. The practitioner asks what you're carrying: stress, grief, physical tension, a sense of disconnection.
  • A settling period. You lie down or sit comfortably, eyes closed, while the practitioner begins channeling energy — hands-on or a few inches above the body, moving through a sequence of positions.
  • The experience itself. Most people report warmth, gentle tingling, waves of calm, or spontaneous emotional release. Some feel very little on the first session and notice effects afterward — better sleep, a lighter mood, less reactive stress.
  • Integration. A good practitioner will talk you through what came up and give you simple ways to carry the effects into your week.

Does distance Reiki actually work?

This is the most common skepticism, and a fair one. The core principle of Reiki treats energy as unbound by physical distance — the same reason two people can feel emotionally connected across continents. In practice, clients consistently report the same depth of sensation and after-effects in a remote session over video as they do in person. What matters most is a quiet space, an undistracted hour, and a practitioner who can hold that space intentionally.

Is Reiki a replacement for medical or mental health care?

No — and any practitioner who tells you otherwise should be a red flag. Reiki is complementary, not alternative. It works well alongside therapy, medication, and conventional treatment, addressing the dimension of stress and energy that clinical care often doesn't reach. Always continue any care your doctor or therapist has prescribed.

How do you know if it's right for you?

Reiki tends to help most with:

  • Chronic stress or a nervous system that won't switch off
  • Grief, emotional overwhelm, or a sense of carrying too much for too long
  • Feeling disconnected from your body or your own intuition
  • Supporting a bigger life transition — alongside therapy, not instead of it

If that sounds familiar, a single session is usually enough to tell you whether the work resonates. For deeper or longer-standing patterns, most practitioners — including Aura Divine — recommend a short arc of three to six sessions so the effects can build.
